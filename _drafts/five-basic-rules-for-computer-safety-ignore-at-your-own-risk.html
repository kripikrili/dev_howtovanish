The Internet plays a large role in many people’s everyday activities -- from searching for movies to buying books, checking bank accounts or credit cards, sending email that lands your in transactional databases. And eEveryday, the threat of hackers, phishers, and other types of scammers increase.

With the use of the Internet and some readily available technology, they scoundrels across the globe are able to trick you into loading their malicious software onto your computer. You need to know how to protect your computer, and this article will get you well on your way.

Some of these nefarious individual’s’ offers may be tempting, but you have to go by the old adage, “If it looks too good to be true, then it probably is.” You also have to be careful where you go while surfing the Internet so that you are able to avoid these pitfalls. There are many ways that you can avoid them risks so long as you are aware that they exist. Fortunately, the vast majority of the time when you surf the Internet, probably 99% of the time, it will be a perfectly safe and enjoyable experience.

SIMPLE RULES

The followingse rules are more applicable to people who use PCs because the Mac has less market share and the operating system is much more secure and therefore the despicable hackers, phishers, and scammers tend to go for the low- hanging fruit found on PCs with outdated software. To make sure that it stays that way you can follow these sets of rules.

First, do is to not mindlessly download or open any attachments when it comes from your email, esp. if the file extenstion is .EXE, .BIN, or .DAT. Even beyond these previously mentioned file extenstions, other types of attachments that are sent by your friends should be scanned by an updated anti-virus program. Two major brands are Norton and McAfee. Some email services, like Yahoo and Gmail, will do this automatically. Your friend may have gotten the file from somewhere that was infected. If the person sending an attachment to you is a stranger -- or a business that you have never heard of -- before then avoid opening the attachment at all cost. There should be no reason that someone is sending you an unsolicited attachment.

Second, is always keep your anti-virus updated. Most new computers will come with an anti-virus already installed. Or you can decide to get the service from another vendor. No matter what you decide make sure that it is always updated. This means that you have to let it run and update everyday. There are new virus definitions that are always released so you have to let the anti-virus program on your system know what they are. If you do not, do this then your anti virus will not catch any newer viruses. Failure to perform regular updatesThis almost makes the anti-virus software useless.

Third, is to always have a firewall up on your system. This is extremely important. A firewall allows you to keep strangers from directly entering your computer without your knowledge. Hackers will probe different IP addresses to find vulnerable machines and systems. If you have a firewall active then your computer will be much less vulnerable. You can use a hardware firewall that comes with your router, for example I have a Linksys router and am extremely satisfied with its capabilities and ease of use, or if you are a little more creative then you can download a software version..

Fourth, is to always update your system operating system software when requested by the software provider. This is as equally important as updating your anti-virus software. Hackers are always looking for holes in computer operating systems (which can circumvent your anti-virus programs), and Windows Operating systems are consistently putting out fixes to holes that are found. However, be aware that hackers and scammers have started using “pop-up” ads through the internet to make you believe that they are a “true Wwindows update”. They are not, the Windows Operating System XP, Vista, and Windows 7 and above come pre-installed with “Windows Update”, which will “pop-up” a little bubble in the lower right corner of your taskbar when Windows/Microsoft has updates for our operating system. This is the only Wwindows update you should use.

Fifth, is to never click on an email that might look like it’s from a person or company that you know but is requesting personal information from you. Many phishers and scammers have started replicating emails that look like they are from your bank or financial company with which you do businessthat you deal with. However, once you click on their links, they take you to their own page and ask you for personal information like bank numbers, social security number, address, phone, etc. These websites can even look like your bank’s website. Do not give any information away to these sites, and most bank policies today state that they will never ask for this information from you via email. If you receive an email that is from your financial institution, call them and don’t supply any information online.
